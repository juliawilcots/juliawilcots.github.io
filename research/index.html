Research

In my research, I use the physical and chemical properties of carbonate sedimentary rocks to infer information about the climates and environments of ancient Earth. I am most interested in the Neoproterozoic Era (1000-540 million years ago), a pivotal time in the history of our planet characterized by global (snowbal Earth) glaciations, large amplitude carbon isotope excursions, and the evolution of animal life. My PhD work culminated in a thesis titled "Dolomite as a Paleoenvironmental Archive," in which I explored the puzzling mineral/rock dolomite (CaMg(CO_3)_2) at a range of spatial and temporal scales. (Dolomite is interesting because it is very common in the rock record and not-at-all common in the modern.) During my postdoc, I will be working on the Bitter Springs Anomaly, the first carbon isotope excursion of the Neoproterozoic Era (ca. 810 Ma) using the high-resolution imaging capabilities of GIRI at Princeton University.
